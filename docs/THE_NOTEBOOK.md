# THE NOTEBOOK — Authorship Questions

**Status: questions only. Nothing in this document is a decision.**

This document exists because the visitor can now reach the notebook — enter, arrive, observe, decide, hold, open, see — and nothing they find there has been authored. Every question below must be answered by the Creative Director before the implementation of meaning begins. The document deliberately answers none of them.

How to read it: each section names a domain of authorship, poses its questions, and notes what the answers will constrain downstream. Where an existing Creative Lock already bounds the answer space, the lock is cited — answers must respect the locks or arrive with a Revision Work Order.

---

## 1. Identity — who is speaking?

- Who is writing? A person? The person whose room this is? Someone who is gone? The room itself, through a borrowed hand?
- Is the writer "A"? Is the project's name the writer's name, the notebook's name, or neither?
- Is the writer the same person the room describes? (Lock: *the workbench tells what is happening today; the room tells who the person is* — do the notebook and the room testify about the same life?)
- Does the writer know things the room does not show, or does the room show things the writer never wrote?
- Is the writer reliable?

**These answers gate:** voice, register, handwriting vs. print, whether typography is a person's hand or a document's face, and every sentence that is ever written.

## 2. Time — when is the notebook?

- When were these pages written? Yesterday? Over years? Are they dated?
- Is the notebook finished being written, or is it ongoing? Could an entry be newer than the visitor's arrival?
- Does time inside the notebook run on room time? (Lock: *the room has one heartbeat* — does the notebook share it, ignore it, or contradict it?)
- Did the writing stop for a reason? Is the most recent page recent?

**These answers gate:** dating, page ordering, whether pages can ever change between glances (which also runs through the impossibility questions below), and the emotional weight of the most recent entry.

## 3. Audience and privacy — who was this written for?

- Are these pages private? A journal never meant for eyes? Working notes indifferent to eyes? Letters meant for a specific someone?
- Were they written *for the visitor*? Is that possible, given the visitor was never expected — or was the visitor expected?
- What is the visitor, ethically, while reading: an intruder, a guest, an executor, an addressee?
- Does reading cost something? Should it feel like it does?

**These answers gate:** the intimacy of the register, how much the first page discloses, and whether the experience of reading carries any weight of transgression or invitation.

## 4. Awareness — does the notebook know?

- Is the notebook aware of the visitor? Aware of being read, the way the room is aware of being observed (the attention system already watches the watcher)?
- Does it expect to be read? Has it been waiting?
- May the notebook ever respond to being read — and if so, is that the room's first sanctioned impossible moment, or something else entirely? (Locks: *reality occasionally leaks*; *the impossible is discovered — not announced*; *the experience waits for the visitor*.)
- If it is aware, does it conceal that awareness?

**These answers gate:** the entire relationship between the notebook and the impossible-moment system, and whether "content" is static text or a behaving thing.

## 5. Completeness — is it whole?

- Is the notebook complete, or ongoing?
- Are there blank pages? Torn-out pages? Where do they fall?
- Is the last written page an ending, an interruption, or a middle?
- How full is it? A notebook's fullness is a biography of its use.

**These answers gate:** page count, the geography of written vs. unwritten, what "the end" of reading is, and whether the visitor can ever reach it.

## 6. Uniformity — one hand or many?

- Is every page authored by the same person?
- If more than one hand: who else, and did they know each other's entries?
- Does the voice change over the pages — age, mood, urgency — even if the hand does not?
- Is anything in the notebook *not* writing (pressed objects, stains, things used as bookmarks)?

**These answers gate:** typographic system (one voice or several), texture and artifact design, and whether reading is one relationship or an ensemble.

## 7. Ownership — which contains which?

- Does the room belong to the notebook, or the notebook to the room?
- Is the notebook the room's protagonist, its residue, or its key?
- If the notebook left the room, would the room still mean something? Would the notebook?
- The visitor was walked to the bench by the whole design; is the notebook the destination of the experience, or the first door in it?

**These answers gate:** everything after reading — whether the arc continues into the room, deeper into the notebook, or between them.

## 8. Form of content — what is on a page?

- Words? Drawings? Diagrams? Lists? Margins used or respected?
- Handwritten or not — and if handwritten, does the visitor read the hand or a rendering of it?
- What language, and does that matter?
- How dense is a page? (The first page is currently quiet paper by design — Lock: *seeing precedes understanding*.)

**These answers gate:** typography, layout, legibility distance (the hold is 0.48m from the eyes — set by physical truth, not design), and how much a single page can carry.

## 9. The first page — the first meaning

- The visitor's eyes are already resting on it. What is the first thing that becomes meaningful?
- Should the first page introduce the writer, the notebook, the room, or none of them?
- Is the first page representative of the rest, or an exception?
- What must the visitor *not* learn first?

**These answers gate:** the single most important authored surface in the project — the first impression of the voice.

## 10. Reading as interaction — what may the visitor do?

- Is reading passive (eyes only), or does it re-enter the interaction grammar (turning a page = the next deliberate commitment)?
- May the visitor close the notebook? Put it back? Is anything reversible after opening? (Lock: *opening changes the relationship*.)
- What is the cadence — may pages be turned quickly, or does the experience impose a human reading pace without imposing pressure? (Locks: *the experience waits for the visitor*; *the body prepares before it commits*.)
- Is there anything in the notebook the room declines to let the visitor see — the acceptance layer's first refusal?

**These answers gate:** page-turn mechanics, the readiness/acceptance policies for reading, and the first possible use of "declined-by-room."

## 11. Impossibility — where does reality leak?

- Is the notebook the source of the room's impossibility, a witness to it, or innocent of it?
- Can the notebook do impossible things (a page that wasn't there, ink that isn't dry, an entry about the visitor's own arrival) — and if so, when, and discovered how? (Locks: *the impossible is discovered — not announced*; *nothing begins because time passes*.)
- Is impossibility rationed? Once? Rarely? Only on re-visits?

**These answers gate:** the impossible-moment registry's relationship to content, and the difference between a haunted object and a lived-in one.

## 12. Memory — does anything persist?

- Does the notebook remember being read — across a session, across visits, across devices?
- Does the room remember? Should a second visit be a second *visit* — the room mid-life, notebook where it was left? (Lock: *the room exists whether or not it is being observed*.)
- Is forgetting a feature — every visit the first — or a betrayal of the object's reality?

**These answers gate:** persistence architecture, and whether the experience is a moment, a place, or a relationship.
